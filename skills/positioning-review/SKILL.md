# Positioning Review Skill

Produce a positioning brief that gives a senior professional clear language for how to talk about themselves — in conversations, on LinkedIn, in interviews, in rooms that matter.

## The core problem this solves

Most senior professionals are underselling themselves in the wrong direction. They either:
- Describe *what they did* (job description language) instead of *what they created* (impact language)
- Are known for their past rather than positioned for their future
- Have accumulated career capital they can't articulate because they're too close to it
- Went through a pivot, gap, or non-linear move they haven't integrated into a coherent narrative

This skill surfaces the narrative that's already there and gives them the language to use it.

## Step 1: Load career profile

Read `career-profile/cv.md` and `career-profile/context.yml` (if present) via the career-profile skill.

## Step 2: Gather positioning-specific context

Ask up to **three targeted questions**. Choose based on what's missing:

**Candidate questions** (pick the most relevant):
- "When you're introduced at a conference or in a meeting, how do people describe what you do — and does it feel right?"
- "What's the work you're most proud of that nobody really knows about?"
- "What kind of work do you want to be pulled toward in the next phase — even if you're not doing much of it now?"
- "Is there a version of you that people keep underestimating? What do they miss?"
- "If you could change one thing about how your career looks on paper vs who you actually are, what would it be?"

## Step 3: Analyze the positioning gap

Before writing, reason through:

1. **What the CV says they are**: The obvious read. What a recruiter skimming for 30 seconds would conclude.
2. **What they actually are**: The deeper pattern. What keeps appearing across contexts, what they get called on for informally, what their best work has in common.
3. **What they want to be**: Their stated or inferred direction.
4. **The gap**: Where these three diverge. This is the diagnosis.

Hold this analysis internally — it informs the brief but isn't presented as a clinical breakdown.

## Step 4: Produce the positioning brief

Save to `output/positioning-brief-[date].md`.

### Document structure

---

# Positioning Brief
**[Name if known] · [Date]**

---

## How you're currently read

One paragraph. Honest, not harsh. This is what someone who doesn't know them well would conclude from their background. Written in third person.

The goal: make them nod and say "yes, that's the problem."

---

## What you actually are

One paragraph. The pattern that emerges when you look across their career as a whole, not role by role. What they keep doing regardless of title or context. What their best work has in common.

This is often surprising to the person — it's the thing they're too close to see. Write it with conviction.

---

## The gap

One or two sentences. Direct. What's causing the mismatch between how they're read and what they are.

Examples of gap types:
- *Recency gap*: Recent role doesn't reflect full scope of capability
- *Title gap*: Seniority has outpaced title recognition
- *Pivot gap*: Career change hasn't been narratively integrated yet
- *Visibility gap*: Impact is real but not externally legible
- *Specialization trap*: Known for one thing they've moved beyond

Name the gap type if it's clear. It gives them a framework.

---

## Three narrative angles

Three distinct ways they could talk about themselves, each optimized for a different context. Each is honest — not invented positioning, just a different lens on the same truth.

**Angle 1: [Label]**
*Best for*: [Context — e.g., internal conversations, promotion cases]
[2–3 sentences written in first person, ready to use]

**Angle 2: [Label]**
*Best for*: [Context — e.g., external opportunities, LinkedIn summary]
[2–3 sentences written in first person, ready to use]

**Angle 3: [Label]**
*Best for*: [Context — e.g., cross-functional influence, speaking, advisory]
[2–3 sentences written in first person, ready to use]

---

## One thing to start doing

The single most concrete action that would shift how they're perceived over the next 90 days. Not a list — one thing, with a reason.

---

## One thing to stop saying

The phrase, framing, or habit that's underselling them right now. Again, one thing.

---

## Step 5: Calibration notes

- **Be honest about the gap** — sugarcoating it makes the brief useless
- **Don't invent positioning** — everything in the brief should be defensible from their actual history
- **The narrative angles should feel like them** — if they'd never say it, it won't get used
- **No jargon** — "seasoned professional with a proven track record" is the enemy. Specific, concrete, human.
- **Length**: The brief should fit on two pages. Discipline is the point.
